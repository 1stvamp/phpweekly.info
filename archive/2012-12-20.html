PHP Weekly

Issue #3 for Thursday 20th of December 2012

In which news is slow but articles are goooood

As we get closer to the festive season proper news and links are beginning to thin out, but quality is an all time high (well for this lists short 3 week life time that is).

I generally encourage everyone to tweet my personal account on Twitter, and follow me if you're that way inclined, but I know my tweets aren't for everyones tastes, being sometimes personal, sometimes about politics, humour, or just non-PHP tech (e.g. Python, JavaScript, DevOps etc.).
With that in mind I created @PHPWeekly in which I will retweet any list related nuggets (new issue announcements for example).

With the 25th being next week (you know, that day involving mince pies) don't expect much in the way of PHP related content, but I will endeavour to get the next issue out as usual on Thursday.
On that note there'll be no RFCs round up this week, partly so I can have a longer section to fill in some content next week, and partly because I seem to be coming down with a cold. *sneeze*

News & announcements

Zend Framework 2.0.6 released
"This release includes almost 60 patches, ranging from minor docblock improvements to complete bugfixes."

Zend Framework 1.11.15 and 1.12.1 released
These are security fix releases for potential exploit found in Zend_Feed_Rss and Zend_Feed_Atom - even if you don't use these classes directly you should still update now in case you rely on anything with a dependency on them or do so in the future.

Wordpress 3.5 "Elvin" released
The biggest change in this fresh release of the defacto blogging app is a reimplemented and smoother photo/media uploading process, an updated default theme and lots of UX tweaks.

Musketeers.me to acquire php|architect’s brand and products effective January 1st
A heartfelt announcement by Marco Tabini on how far he and the team have come with php|a over the past 10 years (as it really been that long? I remember my first subscription dropping through the letterbox like it was yesterday) and how they feel it's time to move on but not without leaving the magazine and accompanying works (books, training, events etc.) in very good community-focussed hands.

php[tek] 2013 announced, and call for papers!
Despite everyone wondering if there would even be a php[tek] next year it turns out things had just been a bit busy at the php|a offices with the above brand changeover news, php[tek] will be back and bigger than ever. No signups yet but they have a CFP out, so get those talk proposals submitted (before Jan 15th).

MongoDB PHP Driver 1.3.2 released

Aura fullstack 1.0.0-beta4 released

Audiovisuals

Voices of the ElePHPant: Interview with Chris Spruck, Kevin Roberts, and Nic Rosental
Cal Evans is back for another excellent podcast, this time featuring a roundtable discussion with PHP Atlanta user group organisers Chris Spruck, Kevin Roberts and Nic Rosental discussing amongst other things the huge success of PHP Atlanta, it's blowout 100th meeting, and the intricacies of what works and doesn't work when organising UGs.


Articles

On Libraries and Dependencies
Bernhard Schussek addresses the recent spate of posts on how framework components, such as Aura should not have dependencies, and with it even more articles and opinion pieces saying that dependencies are a bad thing. Bernhard rightly puts this hogwash to bed before everyone gets all Not Invented Here, as projects like Guzzle and Laravel have recently shown dependencies on existing well written packages are a good thing and with Composer inclusion and use of such packages is practically painless.

A Response to "Micro-Optimizations in PHP"
A detailed response by Jason McCreary, to an earlier linked to article on micro-optimisations, debunking in turn each myth and wrong assumption made. For more information on the single quotes vs double quotes myth Nikita Popov wrote an excellent piece earlier in the year.

Tweetable PHP Non-Alpha
and PHP  non alpha numeric 7 and 6 char code
A very fun couple of posts, by Gareth Heyes and Alex Inführ respectively, on writing PHP code using non-alphanumeric character sets and language tricks. Most of these only work in PHP >= 5.4, but they're very cool from a..well..that's quite cool standpoint, you'd probably never use any of the tricks but it's good as an intellectual exercise to understand them in the context of the language.

Seven Ways To Screw Up BCrypt
List favourite Anthony Ferrara gives a nice rundown of common and some not-so-common pitfalls when generating BCrypt hashes in your application, e.g. for passwords. I particularly liked using versions of PHP with fallbacks if bcrypt  support isn't compiled in and the bonus guarding against timing attacks.

Multiple inheritance with PHP and Traits
Gonzalo Ayuso's post shows how you can kinda do multiple inheritance using PHP 5.4's traits feature, with a good description of what traits are and aren't, and how to use them without method and property collisions.

Timezones, the Right Way
A well written piece on getting those tricky little buggers timezones right in your app. I don't know a single dev who likes working with timezones or the annoyances that go with them, but there is some good solid advice in this article from setting a default timezone and storing all your dates in UTC, to some well thought out [My]SQL queries.

What to Expect From PHP 5.5
Another article on the hotness coming in 5.5, this time from Gabriel Manricks. If you've followed my previous mailshots you'll probably know all these, but Gabriel gives a very thurough write up and covers the lesser known cURL tweaks coming in 5.5, and small but lovely addition of ["const", "and", "string dereferencing"][1].

The Three Ugly Sisters
Pádraic Brady with a brilliant piece of Web Advent on what he terms the Three Ugly Sisters or in other words 3 different classes of security exploit which he's tried to highlight over the year, including Peerjacking, XML injection and our old friend XSS. With each description he includes examples of the wrong way to do it in PHP and how to get it right.

Composer, Github and Travis-CI
A quick and simple post by one of the guys at EasyBib on how they use Composer with Travis-CI to test code on GitHub repos using a stored token. This is similar to how we're doing builds at SD but follow the words of caution closely and don't accidentally leak GitHub tokens in your public repos!

Continuous Deployment Practices
Continuing my practice of ending this section with a not-strictly-PHP entry, another WebAdvent post this time by community-fave Laura Thomson on a subject close to my heart: Continuous Deployment. Laura goes through some of what she considers best practices (and I agree, in some cases I'd say requirements, but that ones a touchy subject). Great advice all round and if you're not doing this then you should..well..you should look into it, anyway. Remember if in doubt just ship it!

Projects

simple-serializer
A nice and simple JSON serializer/deserializer API for handling objects given configuration on how to handle non-JSON-native types.

lf5php
A framwork for creating logging framework facades, or wrappers, so that you can use a different logging mechanism as a backend but access the logging through the same interface, e.g. $logger->info('Info yo!');

xmldsig
A library for handling XML Digital Signatures in PHP.

phpctagger
A utility for generating ctags from a Composer package or project, which you can then use where ever you can use ctags, like in your IDE of choice (and have the unique symbols for the package recognised by the editors highlighting and autocompletion).

PHPHandles
A wrapper around the V8 JavaScript engine and Handlebars.js for generating content from Handlebars templates on the server-side.

Expressive Date
A simple little extension to the inbuilt DateTime class which adds fluent access to methods.

Throttle
A simple library for limiting a function call based on calls within a set time period.

Popcorn
A micro-framework for creating RESTful webservices, based on the Pop framework.

ServeMI
A nice idea that has been done before in other languages and frameworks: take a command line utility, stick a simple PHP script with a given API in front of it, et voila you can now access the util via HTTP. No license or much docs (yet) but interesting to see as a concept.

Pharen
Ever wished you could write your apps in LISP-like syntax and then have it compiled to PHP to run on your server? Well, umm, me either, but Tamreen Khan did and he's done some interesting things with it.
